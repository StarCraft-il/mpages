The slow art of reading in a distracted age

By the Editors · Updated this morning

In a world engineered for constant interruption, sitting with a piece of long-form writing has become almost countercultural. Push notifications, algorithmic feeds, and infinite scroll have rewired our attention spans — and not for the better. Auto-scroll is a small act of reclaiming that lost depth.

The idea is simple: instead of manually dragging your thumb upward every few seconds, you set a pace and let the text come to you. Your hands rest. Your eyes settle. Your mind, freed from the mechanical task of scrolling, can finally give itself over to meaning.

"When the text moves at your reading speed, comprehension doesn't just improve — it transforms. You begin to hear the writer's voice."

Speed and the brain

Reading speed is not a fixed trait. It fluctuates by time of day, subject matter, fatigue, and mood. Dense academic prose demands a crawl; a breezy travel essay invites a glide. The speed slider lets you match the pace to the moment, not to some average human norm.

Research in cognitive psychology suggests that controlled, predictable text motion reduces the cognitive overhead of self-paced reading. When you don't have to decide when to scroll, the decision-making parts of your brain quiet down, and the language-processing parts can take centre stage.

Why smoothness is not cosmetic

A scroll that jumps in discrete ticks forces the visual system to perform micro-saccades — rapid, involuntary eye movements — to re-anchor on the text. Over minutes, this creates a subtle fatigue. Smooth, continuous motion removes those micro-saccades entirely. The eye doesn't chase; it leads.

The smoothness control blends each scroll step into the last using linear interpolation — an easing function borrowed from animation. The result feels like the page is breathing rather than lurching. At high smoothness, there is a gentle inertia: the scroll accelerates softly as it begins and decelerates as it approaches a stop, like water finding its level.

"Smooth motion is not a luxury. It is the difference between reading that feels effortless and reading that quietly exhausts you."

The discipline of a set pace

There is an unexpected benefit to surrendering control of the scroll speed: it makes skimming harder. When text moves at a deliberate pace, the temptation to race ahead diminishes. You are held gently accountable to the paragraph in front of you, the way a guided museum tour holds you to each room before moving to the next.

Writers, particularly essayists and novelists, structure sentences and paragraphs to be experienced in sequence. Auto-scroll honours that structure. The rhythm of a sentence is not just its grammar — it is also its timing, the breath between clauses, the pause after a colon. Moving at a measured pace, you begin to feel that rhythm as the author intended it.

Practical advice for long sessions

Start slower than you think necessary. Most people overestimate their comfortable reading speed on a screen, especially at night or in low light. Set the speed one notch below where you feel impatient; after a few paragraphs, you will rarely want to go faster.

Keep smoothness high for narrative or literary writing, where the flow of prose is part of the experience. For technical documentation or structured lists, you can lower smoothness — the slight jitter actually helps the eye catch discrete items, like bullets or code lines, as distinct units.

Pause freely. Auto-scroll is not a deadline. The pause button exists precisely so you can linger on a sentence, look up a word, or simply breathe. Resume when you are ready. The text is patient.

A quiet resistance

Choosing to read slowly is, in a modest way, a political act. It asserts that not all information needs to be consumed instantly. That some ideas require incubation time. That the writer's craft — the choice of this word over that one, the placement of a paragraph break — deserves to be noticed, not skipped past.

Auto-scroll does not make you read faster. It makes the act of reading feel less effortful, so you are more willing to read at all. And that willingness — to sit with something for ten or twenty or forty minutes — is the rarest digital resource we have left.
